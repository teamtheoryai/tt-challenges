# Jobs to be done

*Fictional. The jobs a fund hires the Second Brain to do, in the order they happen.*

Framed as "when… I want to… so I can…" — the customer's job, not the product's feature.

## JTBD 1 — Define the role

> When a portfolio company needs a new leader, I want to turn a vague need ("we need a CFO for Cascade") into a precise, agreed definition of success, so I can hire against a standard instead of a gut feeling.

The output is a **scorecard**: the mission of the role, the 3–6 measurable outcomes expected in the first 12–18 months, the competencies that predict those outcomes, and the red flags that disqualify. Most searches that fail, fail here — the role was never defined, so "good candidate" was never definable.

## JTBD 2 — Assess candidates against it

> When I'm evaluating people, I want every candidate measured against the same scorecard on real evidence, so I can compare them honestly and see where each one is strong, weak, or unproven.

The product captures interviews and reference calls, scores each candidate on each competency, and — critically — surfaces **where the evidence is thin, where interviewers disagreed, and which claims aren't corroborated.** It does not rank-and-pick. It makes the human's judgment better, not automatic.

## JTBD 3 — Decide

> When it's time to choose, I want a clear, evidence-backed read I can put in front of a deal partner, so we make the call with our eyes open and no one is surprised later.

The output is the **assessment** — the document a deal partner reads before approving a hire. Its job is honesty: strengths, risks, and the specific things still unknown. A good assessment makes a *confident* decision possible, including a confident "no."

## JTBD 4 — Defend the decision

> When I take a hire (or a portfolio-talent call) to the investment committee, I want every claim to trace back to its source, so I can stand behind it when a partner pushes and not get caught out by a number I can't explain.

This is the **defensibility** job, and it's where trust is won or lost. A score that can't be traced is worse than no score — it looks rigorous and isn't. "Defensible at IC" (the Part 2 brief) lives here: the reader must be able to interrogate the conclusion, not just receive it.

## JTBD 5 — Monitor portfolio talent

> When I look across the whole portfolio, I want to know where leadership is strong, where it's a risk, and who's ready for more, so I can act before a weak team costs us the deal.

The output is the **talent review**: every exec across every portco, scored, with flight risk and high-potential flagged, mapped to each company's value-creation plan. Read at board meetings and portfolio reviews. This is the compounding-memory payoff — it's only possible because every prior search fed it.

## The through-line for an engineer

These five jobs are one continuous chain — **define → assess → decide → defend → monitor** — and the currency running through all of them is **trustworthy, traceable evidence.** Every job degrades the same way: if the evidence behind a score can't be trusted or traced, the scorecard is theatre, the assessment misleads, the decision is blind, the IC defense collapses, and the talent review reports fiction with confidence. When you're deciding what "working" means in this repo — or what to build in Part 2 — this chain is why provenance is the product, not a nice-to-have.
