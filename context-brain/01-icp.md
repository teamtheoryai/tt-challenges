# Ideal customer profile

*Fictional. Describes the kind of fund that buys and uses the Second Brain.*

## Who buys

Mid-market private-equity buyout funds — roughly **$500M–$5B in assets under management**, holding **10–40 portfolio companies** at a time. Large enough to run several leadership searches a year across the portfolio; small enough that they don't have a 20-person in-house talent team doing it by hand.

The economic buyer is almost always the fund's **talent function** — an Operating Partner for Talent, a Head of Talent, or a Chief Talent Officer. In smaller funds it may be a deal partner who has informally owned "people" for years.

## Why they buy

- **The stakes are enormous and the tooling is nonexistent.** A fund will underwrite every financial assumption about a company it buys with world-class software, then make the single highest-leverage decision — who runs it — on gut feel captured in scattered documents and someone's memory.
- **A leadership mis-hire is catastrophic.** Replacing a portfolio CEO mid-hold burns 12–18 months and can erase a chunk of the fund's return on that deal. Most funds replace the majority of their CEOs and CFOs at least once during a hold.
- **Every search starts from zero.** The reference notes from the last CFO search are in someone's inbox. The Second Brain makes the fund's own hiring history a compounding asset.
- **They have to defend decisions.** A talent partner answers to an investment committee and to LPs. "We felt good about her" doesn't survive that room; evidence does.

## What a great-fit customer looks like

- Runs a **repeatable, portfolio-wide** talent motion (not one-off founder hiring).
- Has a **named person accountable for talent** who feels the pain personally.
- Already spends real money on external search and assessment ($150K+ per senior search) and knows that spend is uneven and un-compounding.
- Values **explainability and audit** — regulated LPs, formal IC processes.
- Sees the portfolio as the unit, not the single deal.

## What a poor-fit customer looks like

- A fund so small it does one senior hire a year — not enough repetition to compound.
- An organization that wants a **verdict machine** ("just tell me who to hire") — the product deliberately won't, and that mismatch sours the relationship.
- Anyone who wants a sourcing tool or an ATS replacement — wrong product.
- A single operating company with no portfolio and no IC — the multi-tenant, cross-portfolio value evaporates.

## The buying pattern

These funds move on **trust and word of mouth**, not cold outreach. A talent partner who gets value tells three peers at other funds. Deals close fast when a respected operator vouches; they stall on security/compliance review (these are careful, regulated buyers) far more often than on price. Passing a fund's security due diligence quickly is often the real unlock.
